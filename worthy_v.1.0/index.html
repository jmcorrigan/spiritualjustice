Justice from on High

In a divided America, religion inspires activists to fight for justice and equity.


By Jack Corrigan and Scott Brown

Members of four different religious communities reflect on their activism and its roots in their beliefs.

Jessie Cummins
Founder, Seminarians for Justice

Jessie Cummins
Founder, Seminarians for Justice


Jessie Cummins’s fight for social justice started, like many, with a cardboard sign. After arriving at Garrett-Evangelical Theological Seminary last November, excited to work in communities but without any connections, Cummins heard about a protest in Chicago. Gathering a group of her fellow seminarians, she headed down, handmade posters in tow. There, she noticed another cardboard sign with the words “Seminarians for Justice”, a community of Christian students and teachers organizing in Chicago. She had found the connection she needed; within a few months, Cummins was a leader of Seminarians for Justice (SFJ) at Garrett-Evangelical.

In an election year where Trump won over white Christians by huge margins, most wouldn’t point to a seminary as a hub for social justice. But Cummins says it doesn’t have to be this way.

“That language of a bigoted, homophobic Christian makes me so incredibly angry because that’s not how Jesus worked in the world, and that’s not how I want to work in the world,” she says.

Since its founding, SFJ has grown to over 30 students at Garrett-Evangelical, looking to engage with social justice in a way that Cummins says the seminary isn’t doing enough. The group works with Chicago-based political organization The People’s Lobby, campaigning around issues such as mass incarceration, higher education and living wages. Cummins says she’s driven by her own experience growing up in poverty, with both parents working minimum-wage jobs to scrape by, and that she wants to change capitalism and inequality so more people don’t go through that.

“I think that protesting is a beautiful sign of worship,” Cummins says. “This is how you get to the vision of the world… to make it a place where justice can reign.”


Tahera Ahmad
Associate Chaplain and Director of Interfaith Engagement, Northwestern University

Tahera Ahmad
Associate Chaplain and Director of Interfaith Engagement, Northwestern University


Not long after the attacks of September 11, 2001, Northwestern’s Muslim chaplain Tahera Ahmad, a senior in high school at the time, stayed after class to talk to her English teacher. She wasn’t sure why he had asked her to talk, but she relaxed when he opened the conversation by telling her if she ever needed someone to talk with, he was there.

He then continued: “If your father beats you at home, or if he forces you to wear that head scarf, we’re all here for you.”

Ahmad walked out of the classroom confused, unsure how to respond to this collision of genuine concern and overt prejudice.

“Post 9/11, I re-examined everything,” Ahmad says. “I was like ‘The world is gonna continue to ask you questions, and you need some answers fast.’ And for me, it wasn’t necessarily about answering other people’s questions, but more about ‘What does my faith mean to me?’”

Ahmad has confronted this question by diving deeply into grassroots justice work, from supporting Muslim social services in Chicago to leading interfaith partnerships combatting anti-Semitism and Islamophobia. As one of the only women in her field, even her daily work as a chaplain is pushing boundaries within her faith.

Despite challenging these norms, Ahmad remains adamant that Islam at its core is a social justice movement.

“I believe that I am asked to do this work,” she says. “This is a responsibility I have because the Quran asks all of humanity to be in service of each other.”


Jessica Hoffen
President, ZOOZ

Jessica Hoffen
President, ZOOZ


When Jessica Hoffen was seven years old, her father was diagnosed with myeloma. She spent much of her childhood in and out of hospitals as her dad was treated, while her mom worked as a hospital administrator to hold on to the insurance that was literally saving their lives.

“Had I been anyone else... I have no idea where my family would be. Certainly in a lot of debt that we wouldn’t have a way of addressing,” Hoffen says. “ I want to make sure the system is working for all of us equally, and not just for people like me who were lucky enough to have a mother with a job and an education.”

Hoffen, a senior at NU, is now co-president of ZOOZ, a group associated with Hillel that engages with social action through a Jewish lens. ZOOZ does both direct service and advocacy, from working with special needs children to hosting panels on refugees and homelessness.

Judaism is a religion based on history passed down through generations, and much of that history has been a fight against oppression, from Jewish slavery in Egypt to Yom Hashoah, the global day of remembrance for the six million Jews murdered in the Holocaust.

In reaction, justice has become a guiding principle for many Jews. Rabbis in the third century taught the concept of tikkun olam, or “repairing the world” through helping humanity, and the Torah instructs Jews to give one-tenth of their income to the poor as tzedakah, a Hebrew word whose root means “justice”.

“There’s this idea that justice and charity is tied,” Hoffen says. “My religion and culture are there to remind me that there are issues of inequity that need to be addressed. You’re not doing anything special by addressing them.”


Arzu Singh and Asha Sawhney
Freshman and Junior, Northwestern University

Arzu Singh and Asha Sawhney
Junior and Freshman, Northwestern University


Asha Sawhney wears a dagger around her neck.

Admittedly, with its 3 inch length and dulled edges, it couldn’t do much damage. But it serves as a reminder for Asha that she is part of a warrior people, and it is her responsibility to fight for the oppressed.

The dagger is called a kirpan, and it is an important symbol of Asha’s faith, Sikhism. Founded in the state of Punjab in India in the 15th century, Sikhism has about 27 million followers, making it the world’s ninth-largest religion.

Sawhney has worked across the social justice spectrum in her four years at Northwestern, from educating about sexual assault to advocating for divestment from companies in occupied Palestine. Arzu Singh, a family friend who is now a freshman at NU, taught workshops on identity in high school, and is now delving into educational inequality.

Justice and service are central teachings of Sikhism, and can be found throughout many of the religion’s practices. Seva, or selfless service, means helping others without reward, such as cleaning the gurdwara, a Sikh house of worship, or serving in soup kitchens. Through langar, or free kitchen, Sikhs to provide free meals to anyone who shows up to the gurdwara. And Guru Nanak, the founder of Sikhism, asserted that all genders are equal, allowing women to lead worship since the religion’s start.

“I just really take pride in the fact that I don’t have to do much interpretation to connect activism to Sikhism,” says Sawhney. “There’s a lot of people who say that all the world’s problems have been caused by religion. That’s motivated me to say ‘No, my religion fights actively for equality.'”
